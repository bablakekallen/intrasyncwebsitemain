Drones in Precast: Inventory Scanning and Quality Inspection

Leveraging UAV Technology for Efficient Manufacturing Operations

December 6, 2024 | 10 min read • Innovation & Operations

Written by the IntraSync Engineering Team | Reviewed by Zachary Frye, CTO & Founder (7+ years precast industry experience)

Drone Technology

Drones are transforming precast manufacturing in surprising ways. Beyond aerial photography, these unmanned systems provide practical solutions for inventory management and quality control—reducing labor costs while improving accuracy and safety. Here's how progressive manufacturers are putting them to work.

The Inventory Challenge in Large Yards

Large precast operations often store thousands of products across acres of outdoor space. Taking accurate inventory traditionally means workers walking the entire yard, manually counting and recording products—a time-consuming process prone to errors and omissions.

Stacked products are particularly problematic. Ground-level workers can't see top-layer inventory without equipment or climbing—both slow and potentially hazardous approaches.

The result is often outdated inventory data, lost products, and inefficient use of storage space. Enter drone technology.

Automated Inventory Scanning

Modern drones equipped with high-resolution cameras can survey entire storage yards in minutes, capturing detailed imagery of every product. When integrated with RFID systems or visual recognition software, this aerial data automatically updates inventory records.

How It Works

Autonomous drones follow pre-programmed flight paths, systematically photographing storage areas. Advanced systems use GPS waypoints and obstacle avoidance to navigate safely around structures and stacked products.

The captured imagery is processed through computer vision algorithms that identify products by shape, size, and embedded visual markers. Each identified product is counted and located, updating inventory databases automatically.

For operations using RFID tags, drones can be equipped with tag readers, scanning hundreds of tagged products in a single flight—far faster than ground-based methods.

Time and Cost Savings

What takes a crew several hours or even days can be completed in 15-30 minutes with a drone. One operator can manage the entire process, freeing other workers for value-adding tasks.

Manufacturers report 70-80% reduction in time spent on physical inventory counts after implementing drone-based systems. For large operations conducting monthly inventory, this translates to hundreds of labor hours saved annually.

Improved Accuracy

Automated systems eliminate human counting errors and ensure complete coverage—no forgotten corners or overlooked stacks. Computer vision doesn't get tired or distracted, providing consistent accuracy across repeated surveys.

The comprehensive imagery also serves as visual documentation, allowing managers to review questionable counts or investigate discrepancies without returning to the yard.

Quality Inspection Applications

Beyond inventory, drones excel at quality inspection tasks—particularly for large products or hard-to-reach areas.

Surface Inspection

High-resolution cameras capture detailed imagery of product surfaces, revealing cracks, spalling, or finish defects. For tall products like architectural panels or bridge beams, drones inspect upper surfaces without scaffolding or lifts.

Thermal imaging cameras detect subsurface issues—delamination, voids, or moisture intrusion—that aren't visible to the naked eye. This capability is particularly valuable for troubleshooting quality issues or validating repair work.

Dimensional Verification

Photogrammetry—creating 3D models from multiple photographs—allows precise dimensional measurements of large products. Drones capture imagery from multiple angles, and software processes these images into accurate 3D representations.

This technology verifies complex geometries or identifies warping and distortion in large panels—tasks that are difficult and time-consuming with traditional measurement tools.

Facility Inspection

Drones inspect facility infrastructure—roofs, overhead cranes, tall structures—safely and efficiently. Regular monitoring identifies maintenance needs before failures occur, preventing costly downtime.

After severe weather, quick aerial surveys assess damage across large facilities, prioritizing repair work and documenting losses for insurance claims.

Safety Benefits

Drone inspections eliminate many hazardous tasks. Workers no longer need to climb stacked products, work from scissor lifts, or access difficult locations to perform inspections or inventory counts.

This reduction in exposure to fall hazards and equipment risks directly improves workplace safety—a critical consideration in manufacturing environments.

Implementation Considerations

Regulatory Compliance

In the United States, commercial drone operations require FAA Part 107 certification for pilots. This involves passing a knowledge test covering airspace regulations, weather, and flight operations.

Operations over people or beyond visual line of sight may require additional waivers. Understand regulatory requirements before implementation—compliance failures risk significant penalties.

Technology Selection

Industrial operations require commercial-grade drones with appropriate sensors and autonomous capabilities. Consumer drones lack the reliability, payload capacity, and software integration needed for professional applications.

Key considerations include:

  • Flight time: 20-30 minutes minimum for adequate coverage of large facilities
  • Camera resolution: High-resolution sensors for detailed inspection imagery
  • Obstacle avoidance: Essential for safe autonomous operation in cluttered environments
  • Weather resistance: Industrial-grade systems operate in wind, light rain, and temperature extremes
  • Payload capacity: Sufficient for specialized sensors (thermal cameras, RFID readers, etc.)

Software Integration

Maximum value comes from integrating drone data with existing management systems. Standalone imagery is useful; automated inventory updates and integrated inspection reports are transformative.

Look for platforms offering APIs or standard data formats compatible with your ERP and production management software.

Cost Analysis

Entry-level commercial drone systems cost $1,500-$5,000. Professional industrial platforms with advanced sensors and autonomous capabilities range from $10,000-$30,000.

Software platforms for data processing and integration add $2,000-$10,000 annually depending on features and user count.

Training and certification costs are minimal—typically $500-$1,500 per operator for Part 107 certification.

ROI calculations should account for reduced labor costs, improved inventory accuracy, eliminated safety incidents, and faster inspection cycles. Most operations see payback within 12-18 months.

Best Practices for Success

Start with defined use cases. Identify specific high-value applications—inventory counts of certain storage areas, inspection of particular product types—rather than trying to apply drones everywhere immediately.

Invest in training. Proper operator training ensures safe, legal, and effective operations. Budget time for practice and skill development beyond minimum certification requirements.

Establish clear procedures. Document flight paths, pre-flight checks, data processing workflows, and emergency procedures. Standardization ensures consistent results and safe operations.

Plan for weather. Drones have weather limitations. Schedule flights during suitable conditions and maintain backup manual processes for critical operations.

Address privacy and security. Establish policies for drone imagery—what's captured, who accesses it, and how it's stored. This protects proprietary information and respects worker privacy.

Future Capabilities

Drone technology continues advancing rapidly. Emerging capabilities include:

  • AI-powered defect detection that automatically identifies and classifies quality issues
  • Indoor navigation systems enabling inspection inside production facilities
  • Automated charging stations allowing continuous operation without human intervention
  • Swarm technology coordinating multiple drones for faster coverage of large areas
  • Advanced sensors including LiDAR for precise 3D mapping and measurement

Taking the First Step

Don't be intimidated by the technology. Start small with a pilot program focused on a single application—perhaps monthly inventory of your largest storage yard or inspection of tall products.

This limited scope allows you to learn the technology, develop procedures, and demonstrate value before expanding to additional applications.

Partner with experienced drone service providers initially if internal capability development seems daunting. Many manufacturers begin by contracting inspection services, then bring operations in-house once they've proven the value.

Drone technology offers precast manufacturers practical solutions to real operational challenges. The systems are proven, affordable, and increasingly easy to deploy. Progressive manufacturers are already reaping benefits in efficiency, safety, and data quality.

The question is whether you'll lead or follow in adopting this transformative technology.
