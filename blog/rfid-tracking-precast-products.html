RFID Tracking for Precast Products: From Plant to Job Site

Implementing Real-Time Visibility Across Your Entire Supply Chain

December 6, 2024 | 11 min read • Technology & Logistics

Written by the IntraSync Engineering Team | Reviewed by Zachary Frye, CTO & Founder (7+ years precast industry experience)

RFID Tracking

Losing track of precast products costs time, money, and customer relationships. RFID technology provides unprecedented visibility and control over your inventory from the moment concrete is poured until products are installed on the job site. Here's how to implement it effectively.

The Tracking Challenge in Precast

Precast manufacturers face unique tracking challenges. Products move through multiple stages—production, curing, finishing, storage, loading, transport, and installation. Each transition point creates opportunities for lost information or misplaced inventory.

Traditional paper-based systems or simple barcode scanning create gaps in visibility. Workers forget to scan, tags become unreadable, and tracking data lags behind reality. The result: time wasted searching for products, shipment delays, and occasional installation of wrong pieces.

RFID (Radio Frequency Identification) solves these problems by providing automatic, real-time tracking without requiring line-of-sight scanning or manual intervention.

How RFID Works for Precast Products

RFID systems consist of three components: tags embedded in or attached to products, readers that detect tags wirelessly, and software that processes tracking data.

For precast applications, rugged passive UHF (Ultra High Frequency) tags work best. These tags have no battery—they're powered by radio waves from readers, making them maintenance-free with indefinite lifespan. They can be read from 20-30 feet away, allowing bulk scanning of multiple products simultaneously.

Tags are typically embedded in concrete during casting or attached using industrial adhesives. Each tag contains a unique identifier linked to detailed product information in your management system.

Implementation Throughout the Production Process

Casting and Initial Tagging

RFID tags are placed during the casting process, either embedded directly in the concrete or attached to embedded hardware. The tag ID is immediately associated with production data: product type, dimensions, mix design, production date, shift, and specific job order.

This initial encoding creates a digital identity for each piece that follows it through every subsequent operation.

Production Tracking

As products move from stripping to curing areas to finishing stations, RFID readers at strategic locations automatically record transitions. No manual scanning required—products are tracked simply by moving through normal workflow.

Quality control data, finish details, and inspection results are linked to each product's RFID record, creating complete manufacturing documentation.

Inventory Management

In the storage yard, RFID provides real-time inventory visibility. Portal readers at yard entrances/exits automatically track product movement. Handheld readers allow workers to quickly locate specific products among hundreds of similar pieces.

Instead of walking the entire yard to compile inventory, a single reader can scan hundreds of tags in minutes, providing accurate stock counts and locations.

Loading and Shipping

During loading, RFID readers verify that correct products are placed on each truck. The system compares products being loaded against the shipping manifest, immediately flagging discrepancies.

This automated verification prevents wrong products from leaving the plant—a costly mistake that traditional visual checks sometimes miss, especially with similar-looking pieces.

Job Site Delivery and Installation

Upon delivery, contractors use handheld RFID readers to verify shipment contents, instantly checking received products against orders. During installation, readers help crews quickly identify and locate specific pieces from inventory on site.

For projects with hundreds of similar but distinct pieces, this capability dramatically reduces installation time and errors.

Key Benefits Beyond Basic Tracking

Automated Documentation

RFID systems automatically generate complete product histories—when cast, where stored, when shipped, who received it. This documentation supports quality assurance, warranty claims, and compliance requirements without additional administrative work.

Cycle Time Reduction

Eliminate time spent searching for products in the yard or verifying loads manually. Studies show RFID implementation can reduce loading time by 30-50% and virtually eliminate yard search time.

Improved Asset Utilization

Accurate real-time inventory enables better production planning and reduces safety stock requirements. You know exactly what you have and where it is, minimizing overproduction and storage congestion.

Enhanced Customer Service

Provide customers with real-time shipment tracking and precise delivery windows. When they call asking about their order, you have instant, accurate answers rather than promising to "check and call back."

Quality Traceability

If quality issues emerge, quickly identify all affected products based on production date, batch, or any other criteria. RFID makes recalls or investigations dramatically faster and more accurate.

Implementation Considerations

Tag Selection and Placement

Choose tags rated for concrete embedment with sufficient read range for your applications. Tag placement affects readability—avoid locations near dense rebar or metal embedments that can interfere with radio signals.

Test tag placement and readability with sample products before full implementation. What works in controlled tests may need adjustment for production conditions.

Reader Infrastructure

Fixed readers at chokepoints (production transitions, yard gates, loading areas) provide automatic tracking. Handheld readers offer flexibility for inventory checks and product location.

Plan reader placement to maximize coverage while minimizing equipment costs. One well-placed portal reader can eliminate need for multiple handheld scanning operations.

Software Integration

RFID provides maximum value when integrated with existing production management and ERP systems. Standalone RFID data is useful; integrated data that automatically updates inventory, triggers notifications, and informs production decisions is transformative.

Ensure your RFID platform can communicate with existing systems through APIs or standard data formats.

Cost Analysis

Tag costs range from $0.15 to $2.00 depending on features and durability. Readers cost $500-$3,000 for handhelds, $1,500-$5,000 for fixed installations. Software licensing varies by platform and user count.

Most manufacturers see ROI within 12-24 months through reduced labor, fewer errors, and improved throughput. Calculate your specific ROI based on current pain points and operational costs.

Common Implementation Pitfalls

Over-complexity. Start with core tracking functionality before adding advanced features. Many implementations fail by trying to do too much initially.

Inadequate testing. Test thoroughly with actual production conditions before full rollout. Lab tests don't reveal real-world interference and readability challenges.

Poor change management. RFID changes workflows. Train workers thoroughly and address concerns about new technology. Worker buy-in is critical for success.

Ignoring maintenance. Readers require periodic calibration and maintenance. Plan for ongoing system management, not just installation.

Future Developments

RFID technology continues evolving. Active tags with sensors can monitor concrete curing conditions, providing data to optimize production schedules. Integration with IoT platforms enables sophisticated analytics on production flow and bottlenecks.

As construction sites increasingly adopt digital tools, RFID becomes part of broader Building Information Modeling (BIM) and project management ecosystems, providing unprecedented supply chain transparency.

Getting Started

Begin with a pilot program on one product line or major project. This limited scope allows you to learn the technology, refine processes, and demonstrate value before company-wide deployment.

Choose high-value applications for initial implementation—complex projects with many similar pieces, high-volume product lines, or operations with persistent tracking problems.

Work with experienced RFID integrators who understand precast operations. Construction-specific expertise matters—consumer-grade RFID won't survive the harsh precast environment.

RFID tracking transforms precast operations from reactive firefighting to proactive control. Products no longer get lost, shipments arrive complete and correct, and your entire team has real-time visibility into production and inventory.

The technology is proven, affordable, and readily available. The question isn't whether RFID makes sense for precast manufacturers—it's how quickly you can implement it before competitors gain the operational advantages it provides.
